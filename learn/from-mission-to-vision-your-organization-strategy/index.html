From Mission to Vision: Your Organization Strategy

OKC fits within the broader strategic context of an organization.

Your organization should have both a mission and a set of values. The mission answers the question “Why do we exist?” The organizational values are traits or qualities that the organization considers worthwhile and define how you should behave. The outer box in diagram represents your mission and values. Your mission and values help the organization understand what “is in” and what “is out” of the box.

Your organization is currently at Point A, or “As-Is” as seen in the diagram. To assess “As-Is”, you need evaluate your industry, the environment, your competition, customer feedback, and internal surveys.

Your organization should also have identified a future state known as Point B, or “To-Be”. This is your organization’s vision and answers the question, “Where are we going?” Point B is 3 to 5 years out.

The company strategy is the plan that maps out the most direct line between Point A and Point B and informs everyone on how we will get there. As part of the strategy, the organization has detailed the steps of the plan known as the strategic objectives. The strategic objectives provide the high-level view of how to achieve the vision broken down into financial, customer, operational excellence, and people objectives. In the diagram, we can categorize strategy execution into three areas:

  1. Stroke of the Pen: A leader can “sign their name” to make it so. This includes strategic acquisitions, additional marketing spend, staff expansion, technology investment, and new process adoption. These items are often necessary but not enough to make a strategy successful.
  2. Maintain the Operations: This is your day job where you spend most of your time. You must take care of customers, sell and deliver additional product and services, manage team members, and establish processes and key metrics. This area is necessary to run your business. However, if you only focus here, your organization will not move forward towards your vision.
  3. Behavior Change: This is the area where most organizational leaders struggle. You must find a way to get your team to do something different. All strategies have one thing in common: they require team members to change their behavior. You are asking them to focus on longer-term objectives instead of their day job. Ultimately, behavior change is the area of focus for OKC.
